I did find myself wishing for some slightly odder monsters, like Doom 2's baddie resurrecting Arch-Vile, or Hexen's Dark Bishop, with it's corner-turning fireballs. That said, there's something particularly spooky about stepping into a room only to hear 'Die, Human', and know there's a rocket with your name on it coming straight at you.

Jazz Club

In the graphics and sound departments, Duke 3D does

very well. The graphics are clear, even in standard VGA mode, and can be switched up to 800x600 mode, for extra clarity, albeit with some drop in frame-rate. You might just get away with standard VGA on a low-end 486 machine, but don't count on it. 486DX2-66 is the minimum you should really try this game on. The music thrums away in the background, without being too obtrusive, and is really something if you've got an AWE32. Yells and booms abound, with Duke piping up with quips every now and then. There's also an added touch of humour in this game. You'll find loads of movie references popping up, from the cocooned girl in the sewers, to the crushed Terminator in the steel mill. There's also loads of extra secrets, if you look hard enough. The game CD also comes with a number of extras, including the full registered versions of Duke Nukem 1 + 2 (okayish platform games), a game editor (good once you get into it, but not intuitive), and a whole bunch of other utilities.
